# Famous Ancient Chinese Battles — Strategic Analysis

## 1. Battle of Red Cliffs (赤壁之戰, 208 CE)
**Combatants:** Cao Cao (northern forces, ~200K) vs Sun Quan-Liu Bei alliance (~50K)
**Terrain:** Yangtze River, winter

**Key Tactics:**
- **Fire attack (火攻):** Huang Gai's fire ships devastated Cao Cao's chained fleet. Wind direction was critical — the southeast wind carried fire into the northern fleet.
- **Chain stratagem (連環計):** Pang Tong convinced Cao Cao to chain his ships together for stability, making them vulnerable to fire.
- **Deception (苦肉計):** Huang Gai feigned surrender to get close with fire ships.

**Alkaid Application:** Fire ships deal 200 base damage, +50% with favorable wind. Chaining ships (staying close) risks area fire spread. Always check wind direction before naval combat. Lesson: terrain and weather can overcome numerical superiority.

## 2. Battle of Guandu (官渡之戰, 200 CE)
**Combatants:** Cao Cao (~20K) vs Yuan Shao (~100K)
**Terrain:** Plains near Yellow River

**Key Tactics:**
- **Supply raid (釜底抽薪):** Cao Cao's decisive move was a cavalry raid on Yuan Shao's supply depot at Wuchao. Destroying the supplies caused Yuan Shao's massive army to collapse.
- **Patience (以逸待勞):** Cao Cao defended for months, waiting for the right opportunity despite being heavily outnumbered.

**Alkaid Application:** Light cavalry can raid supply depots (structure HP 500). Destroying the depot eliminates all food instantly. This is often more effective than winning a direct battle. Supply factor is 20% of surrender pressure — combine with other factors for rapid collapse.

## 3. Battle of Changping (長平之戰, 260 BCE)
**Combatants:** Qin (~550K) vs Zhao (~450K)
**Terrain:** Mountainous terrain, narrow valleys

**Key Tactics:**
- **Feigned retreat (詐敗):** Qin general Bai Qi faked retreat to lure Zhao forces from their defensive positions.
- **Encirclement (圍):** Qin cavalry cut behind Zhao forces, encircling them completely.
- **Starvation siege:** Cut off from supplies for 46 days, the Zhao army starved and surrendered.

**Alkaid Application:** Encirclement factor (15% of surrender pressure) combined with supply starvation (20%) creates overwhelming pressure. A fully encircled, starving army reaches 80 pressure rapidly. This was the largest battle of the Warring States period.

**Aftermath:** Bai Qi executed 400K surrendered soldiers. In Alkaid, the clemency system reflects this — rejecting surrender makes future enemies fight 20% harder.

## 4. Battle of Changshao (長勺之戰, 684 BCE)
**Combatants:** Lu (smaller) vs Qi (larger)
**Terrain:** Open plains

**Key Tactics:**
- **Morale exhaustion (一鼓作氣):** Advisor Cao Gui waited through three of Qi's drum charges before counterattacking, letting Qi's morale exhaust through repeated failed assaults.

**Alkaid Application:** Extended combat causes -0.5 morale/tick after 30 ticks. An attacking force that fails to break through quickly will lose morale and fatigue, while the defender recovers. Patience is a weapon.

## 5. Battle of Fei River (淝水之戰, 383 CE)
**Combatants:** Eastern Jin (~80K) vs Former Qin (~870K)
**Terrain:** Fei River crossing

**Key Tactics:**
- **Exploiting retreat order confusion:** Jin requested Qin pull back from the river to allow a proper battle. When Qin forces began to withdraw, a false "retreat" shout caused panic and a rout cascade.

**Alkaid Application:** Rout cascade mechanics: when 30%+ of an army is routing, -20 morale to all remaining units. When 50%+, -40 morale causes total collapse. A small trigger can destroy a massive army. Order confusion during withdrawal (morale already shaken) is devastating.

## 6. Battle of Mailing (馬陵之戰, 341 BCE)
**Combatants:** Qi vs Wei
**Terrain:** Narrow mountain pass with forests

**Key Tactics:**
- **Ambush (伏擊):** Sun Bin (descendant of Sun Tzu) set an ambush in the narrow Mailing pass. He reduced the number of campfires each night to feign desertions, luring Wei general Pang Juan into pursuit.
- **Terrain advantage:** The narrow pass negated Wei's numerical advantage. Crossbowmen hidden in the forests rained arrows on the trapped column.

**Alkaid Application:** Forest terrain gives +25% defense and -60% range for ranged units... unless you're the one hiding IN the forest. Ambushes from fog of war deal +50% first-strike damage and -15 morale shock. Night ambushes double the effect.

## 7. Battle of Xiangyang (襄陽之戰, 219 CE)
**Combatants:** Guan Yu (Shu-Han) vs Cao Ren (Wei)
**Terrain:** Flooding Han River, besieged city

**Key Tactics:**
- **Flooding (水攻):** Guan Yu used the autumn floods of the Han River to drown Wei reinforcements under Yu Jin. Seven armies destroyed by water.
- **Siege and attrition:** Long siege of Xiangyang/Fancheng, relying on cutting supplies and reinforcements.

**Alkaid Application:** Weather matters enormously. Rain increases river ford danger (+50% drowning chance), reduces ranged effectiveness, and slows movement. Siege Engineers (range 10, area damage) can bombard cities from safety, but need protection from cavalry raids.

## 8. Zhuge Liang's Northern Expeditions (北伐, 228-234 CE)
**Five campaigns, all ultimately failed**

**Key Lesson: Supply lines decide wars**
- The Qinling Mountains made supply delivery nearly impossible
- Even with superior tactics, Zhuge Liang had to retreat when food ran out
- His invention of the "wooden ox and gliding horse" (木牛流馬) was an attempt to solve the supply problem

**Alkaid Application:** The supply system directly mirrors this. An army cannot fight without food. Supply line length, terrain, and vulnerability to cavalry raids determine whether an offensive is sustainable. Always secure your supply before advancing.

## 9. Battle of Bowang (博望之戰, 202 CE)
**Combatants:** Liu Bei/Zhuge Liang vs Xiahou Dun (Wei)
**Terrain:** Forest with narrow approach

**Key Tactics:**
- **Fire ambush (火計):** Zhuge Liang's first major victory. Used a feigned retreat to lure Xiahou Dun's forces into a forest, then set fires to trap them.
- **Prepared battlefield:** Pre-positioned troops at key chokepoints before the battle began.

**Alkaid Application:** Deployment phase positioning is crucial. Place Dao Swordsmen in forests (they get +15% damage, no movement penalty). Use the terrain to your advantage before the first clash. Forest fires from Gong Archers' fire arrows can change the battlefield.

## 10. Wu Hu Campaign — Siege of Chengdu (成都之戰, 214 CE)
**Combatants:** Liu Bei vs Liu Zhang
**Terrain:** Sichuan basin, fortified cities

**Key Tactics:**
- **Political warfare alongside military:** Convinced enemy generals to defect through promises of mercy
- **Multi-axis advance:** Attacked from multiple directions simultaneously

**Alkaid Application:** The clemency system rewards accepting surrenders — captured troops at 50% effectiveness join your army, and future enemies are 10% more likely to surrender. Mercy is strategically superior to massacre in a long campaign.

---

## Strategic Principles from History

1. **Supply wins wars** — Guandu, Northern Expeditions. Cut the enemy's food.
2. **Terrain is everything** — Changping, Mailing, Bowang. Fight where you're strong.
3. **Morale breaks armies** — Fei River, Changshao. Patience causes collapse.
4. **Fire is devastating** — Red Cliffs, Bowang. Combine with wind and terrain.
5. **Intelligence wins battles** — Scouts prevent ambush, enable surprise.
6. **Numbers don't guarantee victory** — Red Cliffs (5:1), Fei River (10:1). Quality, terrain, and strategy matter more.
7. **Mercy is strategic** — Cao Cao absorbed defeated armies. Bai Qi's massacre made all future enemies fight to the death.
